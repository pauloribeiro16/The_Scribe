According to Article 17 of the GDPR, a person can request the erasure of their data under the following conditions:

**1. Legitimate Interest:** This is the primary basis for erasure requests.  The data subject has a legitimate interest that outweighs the public interest in the processing of their data. This interest can relate to, for example, the individual’s specific situation, or the data’s long-term storage.

**2. Legal Obligation:** The data subject has a legal obligation to conserve the data.  This typically arises from a legal requirement, such as a contractual obligation or a judicial order.

**3. Explicit Consent:**  The data subject has given explicit consent for the processing of their data. This consent must be freely given, specific, informed, and unambiguous.  It also must be freely given and not induced by any pressure or coercion.

**4. Legal Requirements:** The data subject has a legal requirement to preserve the data. This might be a specific legal obligation relating to a past fact or a duty to comply with a legal obligation.

**5. Lack of Legitimate Processing Purpose:**  The data controller (the organization processing the data) has ceased the legitimate processing purposes for which the data was collected. This means that the data is no longer needed for the original purpose and no longer has value for the data subject.
